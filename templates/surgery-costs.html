Before the sticker shock

Patterns that show up again and again in these reports, worth knowing before your consult, and before you open the first bill. Tap to expand.

"Billed" is not what you owe

Insurers here were billed $165k, $300k, $400k, $1M, even $1.5M and $2.5M in disputed cases that resolved to a few thousand dollars or less, while those same insured patients ultimately paid between $0 and $30,000, most well under $7,000. One surgery was billed at $400,000 where the surgeon's own cash price was $15,000; a commenter who works in insurance verification adds that insurers' contracted rates can shrink even scary plan math to a few hundred dollars. The giant number on an EOB is a provider-insurer negotiation, not your bill.

Your deductible and out-of-pocket max are the real numbers

Insured patients' payments tracked their plan, not the surgery: $0 with a met out-of-pocket max, $10-$300 copays at in-network hospitals, deductibles around $2,000-$6,500, and up to $14k-$30k on high-deductible or out-of-network plans. One patient's second surgery cost $0 purely because her plan year hadn't reset. Look those two figures up before anything else.

In-network excision specialists do exist

NYU Langone patients here paid $90-$300 copays, Portland patients with the same insurer were quoted $680-$5,000, and one patient found the in-network head of a hospital endo center through personal recommendations. An insurance-verification worker suggests combing your insurer's own directory and searching each listed surgeon for excision experience. Tedious, but it beats assuming cash-pay is the only route.

Anesthesia, co-surgeons and facilities can arrive out-of-network

Surprise bills came from anesthesiologists patients never chose; assistant-surgeon fees ($2,500), anesthesiologist charges ($20-30k billed) and follow-up imaging (an ultrasound billed at ~$10k) each appeared as separate line items. In the US, the No Surprises Act (2022) protects against many out-of-network bills at in-network facilities. Ask about it, and ask up front exactly who else will bill you.

Self-pay: get every number in writing, early

Self-pay discounts can be dramatic ($15k versus $40k+ at the same hospital) but fragile; one billing office wrongly attached insurance info that would have voided the discount. Another patient saved for an $8k surgeon fee, only for the hospital to demand $47,000 on the day of surgery and refuse service. Get the complete facility + anesthesia + surgeon total in writing well before your date.

Payment plans, financing and aid are everywhere; ask

Practices here offered installments on balances from $2,500 to $30,000, sometimes at 0% for as long as you need; hospital financial aid took an uninsured patient's $50k bill down to about $5k, and erased another's $2,500 balance entirely based on income. Others used CareCredit-style financing, county programs, or Medicaid. Research hospitals with their own out-of-pocket caps also appear in these reports.

Disputes take time, and persistence works

Several reports describe roughly a year of claims back-and-forth before balances of $1.5M and $2.5M shrank to almost nothing; another patient's denied $320k claim is being fought by an external appeals agency. Keep every document, request itemized bills, audit charges while claims are still processing, get pre-approval in writing, appeal, escalate.

Quotes aren't apples-to-apples, and the facility drives the bill

Self-pay figures run from a $6,500 surgeon-only fee to $60,000 all-in. Where you have the surgery matters enormously: in one fully itemized $51,512 bill, the one-night hospital stay was $41k of it, while a patient whose surgeon uses an ambulatory surgery center paid $1,700-$2,300 per lap. Ask exactly what's included.

Public systems: free or near-free, but the wait is the price

Reports from the UK, Canada, Ireland, France, Germany, Denmark, Finland, Belgium, Lithuania, Australia and New Zealand ran from $0 to a few hundred dollars, but the waits ranged from 2 months to 3+ years. Many patients chose the wait deliberately over debt; others went private or traveled for speed.

The bill isn't the whole cost

One patient lost almost $18,000 of income in a year from being too sick to work, on top of her $9,500 surgery. Others paid for travel, uncovered medications, and rule-out procedures. When you're budgeting, count recovery time, lost wages and the diagnostic runway, not just the surgeon's quote.

About this tracker What this is: a community-gathered collection of real endometriosis surgery costs, inspired by salary-transparency projects. Pay transparency changed how people negotiate work; cost transparency can do the same for endo care, where pricing is routinely gatekept until the bill arrives.

What this isn't: verified, complete, or advice. Figures are unedited beyond formatting, may mix years and currencies, and reflect individual plans, hospitals, and negotiations that won't match yours exactly. Nothing here is medical, financial, or legal advice; always confirm costs directly with your provider and insurer before making decisions.

Where surgeons are named, entries describe billing experiences, not surgical skill or quality of care. Names appear because patients chose to share them, exactly as they would in a support group.
