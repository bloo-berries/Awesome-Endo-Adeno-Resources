## Medication Guide

A comprehensive comparison of medications commonly used in the management of endometriosis and adenomyosis. **Always consult your healthcare provider before starting, stopping, or changing any medication.**

## Hormonal Treatments

Hormonal therapies reduce estrogen levels or counteract its effects, slowing endometriosis growth and reducing symptoms.

| Name | Type | Mechanism | Common Side Effects |
|---|---|---|---|
| Combined Oral Contraceptives | Estrogen + Progestin pill | Suppresses ovulation, reduces menstrual flow | Nausea, breast tenderness, mood changes |
| Norethindrone (Aygestin) | Progestin (oral) | Thins endometrial tissue | Irregular bleeding, weight gain, mood changes |
| Medroxyprogesterone (Depo-Provera) | Progestin (injection) | Suppresses ovulation and endometrial growth | Weight gain, bone density loss, irregular bleeding |
| Levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena) | Progestin (intrauterine) | Local progestin effect on endometrium | Irregular bleeding initially, headache |
| Dienogest (Visanne) | Progestin (oral) | Directly suppresses endometriotic tissue | Headache, irregular bleeding, mood changes |
| Elagolix (Orilissa) | GnRH antagonist (oral) | Partially suppresses estrogen | Hot flashes, night sweats, bone density loss |
| Relugolix combo (Myfembree) | GnRH antagonist + add-back | Suppresses estrogen with hormonal add-back | Hot flashes, mood changes (milder with add-back) |
| Leuprolide (Lupron) | GnRH agonist (injection) | Suppresses estrogen (medical menopause) | Hot flashes, bone loss, mood changes, joint pain |
| Nafarelin (Synarel) | GnRH agonist (nasal spray) | Suppresses estrogen production | Hot flashes, nasal irritation, headache |
| Danazol | Androgenic steroid | Creates high-androgen environment | Weight gain, acne, voice deepening (rarely used) |

## Pain Management

| Name | Type | Mechanism | Common Side Effects |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) | NSAID | Reduces prostaglandins and inflammation | GI upset, ulcer risk long-term |
| Naproxen (Aleve) | NSAID | Longer-acting anti-inflammatory | GI upset, cardiovascular risk long-term |
| Mefenamic acid (Ponstel) | NSAID | Targets menstrual pain pathways | GI upset, diarrhea |
| Acetaminophen (Tylenol) | Analgesic | Central pain pathway modulation | Liver risk at high doses |
| Gabapentin (Neurontin) | Neuromodulator | Reduces nerve pain signaling | Drowsiness, dizziness, weight gain |
| Pregabalin (Lyrica) | Neuromodulator | Reduces nerve pain signaling | Drowsiness, dizziness, edema |
| Amitriptyline | Tricyclic antidepressant | Low-dose pain and sleep modulation | Drowsiness, dry mouth, constipation |
| Duloxetine (Cymbalta) | SNRI antidepressant | Dual pain and mood modulation | Nausea, dizziness, fatigue |

## Surgical Options

Surgical approaches are often part of the treatment plan. See [Diagnosis & Treatments](/diagnosis/) for full surgical details.

| Approach | Type | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| Laparoscopic excision | Gold standard, removes lesions including roots | 2–6 weeks |
| Laparoscopic ablation | Surface burning of visible lesions (higher recurrence) | 1–4 weeks |
| Robotic-assisted excision | Enhanced precision for complex cases | 2–6 weeks |
| Hysterectomy | Removes uterus (helps adenomyosis, does not cure endo) | 4–8 weeks |
| Oophorectomy | Removes ovaries, induces menopause (last resort) | 4–8 weeks |

## Complementary Approaches

Not replacements for medical treatment, but may help manage symptoms alongside conventional care:

| Approach | Type | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pelvic floor physiotherapy | Physical therapy | Addresses muscle tension, pain, and dysfunction |
| Acupuncture | Traditional medicine | May help with pain management |
| TENS | Pain device | Non-invasive nerve stimulation for pain |
| Anti-inflammatory diet | Nutrition | May reduce inflammation; results vary |
| CBD products | Supplement | Limited research; legal status varies |
| Turmeric/Curcumin | Supplement | Anti-inflammatory; limited endo-specific evidence |

## Important Notes

- **No medication cures endometriosis.** They manage symptoms and may slow progression
- **Hormonal treatments suppress, not eliminate.** Symptoms typically return when stopped
- **Side effect profiles vary.** What works for one person may not work for another
- **Bone density monitoring** is essential for GnRH agonists/antagonists used beyond 6 months
- **Add-back therapy** (low-dose estrogen/progesterone) can reduce menopause-like side effects of GnRH treatments
- **Discuss fertility plans** with your doctor before starting hormonal therapy, as some treatments suppress fertility
- **Multi-modal approach** works best, combining medication, surgery, physiotherapy, and lifestyle changes
