## Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about Endometriosis and Adenomyosis.

### About the Conditions

<details>
<summary>What is Endometriosis?</summary>

A chronic condition where tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus, causing inflammation, pain, and scar tissue. See [Endometriosis](/endometriosis/) for full details.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is Adenomyosis?</summary>

A condition where endometrial-like tissue grows into the muscular wall of the uterus, causing enlargement, heavy periods, and pain. See [Adenomyosis](/adenomyosis/) for full details.

</details>

<details>
<summary>How common is Endometriosis?</summary>

Approximately 10% of women and girls of reproductive age, around 190 million worldwide according to the WHO. In Australia, the figure is estimated at 1 in 7, suggesting global underdiagnosis.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is the difference between Endometriosis and Adenomyosis?</summary>

Endometriosis grows outside the uterus; Adenomyosis grows into the uterine wall. Both cause pain and heavy bleeding, and many people have both.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can men get Endometriosis?</summary>

Extremely rare, but documented in cisgender men. It primarily affects people with female reproductive organs, including transgender men and non-binary individuals.

</details>

### Symptoms

<details>
<summary>What are the main symptoms of Endometriosis?</summary>

Severe period pain, chronic pelvic pain, pain during sex, painful bowel movements/urination, heavy periods, fatigue, bloating ("endo belly"), and difficulty conceiving. See [Endometriosis](/endometriosis/#endometriosis-symptoms-by-stage) for a full symptom table by stage.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Is severe period pain normal?</summary>

No. Pain that interferes with daily activities or isn't relieved by over-the-counter medication is not normal and should be evaluated. Severe period pain is the most common symptom of Endometriosis.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is "endo belly"?</summary>

Severe abdominal bloating that can make the stomach appear significantly larger, sometimes lasting hours or days. Often worse around menstruation.

</details>

### Diagnosis

<details>
<summary>How is Endometriosis diagnosed?</summary>

Through clinical assessment, transvaginal ultrasound, and MRI. The gold standard remains surgical confirmation via laparoscopy. See [Diagnosis](/diagnosis/) for current methods and emerging tests.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Why does diagnosis take so long?</summary>

On average 7-10 years, because symptoms are dismissed as "normal period pain," overlap with other conditions (IBS, UTIs), and there is no simple blood test.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can Endometriosis be detected by ultrasound?</summary>

Transvaginal ultrasound can detect deep infiltrating Endometriosis and endometriomas, but superficial disease is usually not visible. A normal ultrasound does not rule out Endometriosis.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Is there a blood test for Endometriosis?</summary>

Not yet. CA-125 is not specific enough. Several promising biomarker tests are in development. See [Diagnosis](/diagnosis/) for emerging diagnostics.

</details>

### Treatment

<details>
<summary>Is there a cure for Endometriosis?</summary>

No cure exists, but symptoms can be managed through excision surgery, hormonal therapy, pain management, and physiotherapy. See [Treatments](/treatments/) for a full overview.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is excision surgery?</summary>

Cutting out Endometriosis lesions completely, including their roots. This is the gold standard, as ablation (burning the surface) may leave disease behind.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Does hysterectomy cure Endometriosis?</summary>

No. The disease exists outside the uterus. Hysterectomy may help with Adenomyosis specifically, but Endometriosis lesions can persist unless excised.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What hormonal treatments are available?</summary>

Combined oral contraceptives, progestins (pills, IUDs, implants), GnRH agonists, and GnRH antagonists. These suppress symptoms but do not eliminate existing disease. See [Medications](/medications/) for details.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can diet help with Endometriosis?</summary>

Anti-inflammatory diets may help manage symptoms. Reducing processed foods, red meat, alcohol, and caffeine while increasing omega-3s, fruits, and vegetables has shown benefit in some studies.

</details>

### Fertility

<details>
<summary>Does Endometriosis cause infertility?</summary>

Endometriosis is found in 25-50% of women experiencing infertility, but many people conceive naturally. Severity does not always predict fertility impact. See [Fertility](/fertility/) for details.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can I get pregnant with Endometriosis?</summary>

Yes. Many conceive naturally. Options for those needing help include IUI, IVF, and surgical treatment. See [Fertility](/fertility/) for guidance.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Should I freeze my eggs if I have Endometriosis?</summary>

It can be a reasonable option, especially if endometriomas are present or ovarian reserve is declining. Discuss timing with a reproductive endocrinologist. See [Fertility](/fertility/#fertility-preservation).

</details>

### Living with Endometriosis

<details>
<summary>How can I find an Endometriosis specialist?</summary>

Look for excision surgery specialists on [iCareBetter](https://icarebetter.com/), [The Yellow Hub](https://www.theyellowhub.org/), or Nancy's Nook. See [Healthcare Providers](/healthcare/) for full listings.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Is Endometriosis a disability?</summary>

In many countries, yes. In the US it may qualify under the ADA; in the UK under the Equality Act 2010. Documentation from your specialist supports workplace accommodations.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Does Endometriosis get worse with age?</summary>

It can be progressive without treatment, but this is not universal. Menopause may reduce symptoms but does not guarantee resolution.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What support is available for people with Endometriosis?</summary>

Online communities, advocacy organizations, mental health resources, and peer support groups. See [Education & Community](/education/) and [Support & Advocacy](/resources/).

</details>
