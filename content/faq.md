## Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about endometriosis and adenomyosis.

### About the Conditions

<details>
<summary>What is endometriosis?</summary>

Endometriosis is a chronic condition where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus (endometrium) grows outside the uterus. It can be found on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, bowel, bladder, and other areas of the pelvis. This tissue responds to hormonal changes, causing inflammation, pain, and scar tissue formation.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is adenomyosis?</summary>

Adenomyosis occurs when endometrial-like tissue grows into the muscular wall of the uterus (myometrium). This causes the uterus to enlarge and can lead to heavy, painful periods. Adenomyosis and endometriosis can occur together.

</details>

<details>
<summary>How common is endometriosis?</summary>

Recent research shows that approximately 1 in 7 women and people assigned female at birth have endometriosis — roughly 570 million people worldwide. Previous estimates of 1 in 10 are now considered conservative based on updated studies.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is the difference between endometriosis and adenomyosis?</summary>

Endometriosis involves tissue growing outside the uterus, while adenomyosis involves tissue growing into the uterine wall. Both cause pain and heavy bleeding, but they affect different locations. Many people have both conditions simultaneously.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can men get endometriosis?</summary>

While extremely rare, there have been documented cases of endometriosis in cisgender men. Endometriosis primarily affects people with female reproductive organs, including transgender men and non-binary individuals assigned female at birth.

</details>

### Symptoms

<details>
<summary>What are the main symptoms of endometriosis?</summary>

Common symptoms include severe period pain (dysmenorrhea), chronic pelvic pain, pain during or after sex (dyspareunia), painful bowel movements or urination, heavy or irregular periods, fatigue, bloating ("endo belly"), and difficulty getting pregnant. Symptoms vary widely between individuals.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Is severe period pain normal?</summary>

No. While mild cramps are common, pain that interferes with daily activities, requires strong painkillers, causes you to miss work or school, or is not relieved by over-the-counter medication is not normal and should be evaluated by a doctor. Severe period pain is the most common symptom of endometriosis.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is "endo belly"?</summary>

"Endo belly" refers to severe abdominal bloating and distension commonly experienced by people with endometriosis. The stomach can appear significantly larger — sometimes resembling a pregnancy — and may last for hours or days. It is often worse around menstruation and can be triggered by certain foods.

</details>

### Diagnosis

<details>
<summary>How is endometriosis diagnosed?</summary>

Historically, laparoscopic surgery was the only definitive way to diagnose endometriosis. However, experienced specialists can now identify endometriosis through detailed clinical assessment, ultrasound (especially transvaginal), and MRI. The gold standard remains surgical confirmation with histological examination.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Why does diagnosis take so long?</summary>

On average, it takes 7-10 years to receive an endometriosis diagnosis. Delays occur because symptoms are often dismissed as "normal period pain," there is limited awareness among general practitioners, symptoms overlap with other conditions (IBS, UTIs), and there is no simple blood test for the disease.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can endometriosis be detected by ultrasound?</summary>

Transvaginal ultrasound can detect deep infiltrating endometriosis and endometriomas (ovarian cysts) when performed by a skilled sonographer. However, superficial peritoneal endometriosis is usually not visible on ultrasound. A normal ultrasound does not rule out endometriosis.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Is there a blood test for endometriosis?</summary>

Currently, there is no reliable blood test to diagnose endometriosis. CA-125 levels may be elevated in some cases, but the test is not specific or sensitive enough for diagnosis. Research into biomarkers is ongoing, and several promising tests are in development.

</details>

### Treatment

<details>
<summary>Is there a cure for endometriosis?</summary>

There is currently no cure for endometriosis. However, symptoms can be managed through various treatments including surgery (particularly excision surgery), hormonal therapies, pain management, physiotherapy, and lifestyle modifications. Treatment plans should be individualized.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is excision surgery?</summary>

Excision surgery involves cutting out endometriosis lesions completely, including their roots. This is considered the gold standard surgical treatment for endometriosis, as opposed to ablation (burning the surface), which may leave disease behind. Excision is best performed by an experienced specialist.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Does hysterectomy cure endometriosis?</summary>

No. Hysterectomy (removal of the uterus) does not cure endometriosis because the disease exists outside the uterus. Hysterectomy may help with adenomyosis symptoms specifically. If endometriosis is not fully excised at the time of hysterectomy, symptoms can persist.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What hormonal treatments are available?</summary>

Hormonal treatments include combined oral contraceptives, progestins (oral, IUD, or injection), GnRH agonists (e.g., Lupron), and GnRH antagonists (e.g., Orilissa/elagolix). These treatments aim to suppress estrogen and slow endometriosis growth, but they do not eliminate existing disease and symptoms often return when treatment stops.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can diet help with endometriosis?</summary>

While diet alone cannot cure endometriosis, anti-inflammatory diets may help manage symptoms for some people. Reducing processed foods, red meat, alcohol, and caffeine while increasing omega-3 fatty acids, fruits, vegetables, and fiber has shown benefit in some studies. Individual responses vary significantly.

</details>

### Fertility

<details>
<summary>Does endometriosis cause infertility?</summary>

Endometriosis is found in 25-50% of women experiencing infertility. It can affect fertility through inflammation, adhesions, anatomical distortion, and changes to the hormonal and immune environment. However, many people with endometriosis conceive naturally. The severity of disease does not always correlate with the degree of fertility impairment.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can I get pregnant with endometriosis?</summary>

Yes. Many people with endometriosis conceive naturally. For those who need assistance, options include fertility medications, intrauterine insemination (IUI), in vitro fertilization (IVF), and surgical treatment of endometriosis. Early consultation with a reproductive specialist is recommended if you are having difficulty conceiving.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Should I freeze my eggs if I have endometriosis?</summary>

Egg freezing can be a reasonable option for people with endometriosis who want to preserve fertility for the future, especially if ovarian endometriomas are present. Discuss timing and options with a reproductive endocrinologist, as endometriosis can progressively affect ovarian reserve over time.

</details>

### Living with Endometriosis

<details>
<summary>How can I find an endometriosis specialist?</summary>

Look for gynecologists who specialize in excision surgery for endometriosis. Resources include iCareBetter, The Yellow Hub, and Nancy's Nook (educational resource with doctor lists). Ask about the surgeon's specific experience with endometriosis excision, their case volume, and whether they operate in a multidisciplinary team.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Is endometriosis a disability?</summary>

In many countries, severe endometriosis can qualify as a disability under employment and disability laws. In the US, endometriosis may qualify under the ADA. In the UK, it can be considered a disability under the Equality Act 2010. Documentation from your specialist can support workplace accommodations.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Does endometriosis get worse with age?</summary>

Endometriosis is a progressive disease for some people, meaning it can worsen over time without treatment. However, this is not universal. Menopause may reduce symptoms due to decreased estrogen, but it does not guarantee resolution. Some people experience persistent symptoms after menopause.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What support is available for people with endometriosis?</summary>

Support options include online communities (Reddit r/Endo, r/Endometriosis), advocacy organizations (Endometriosis Foundation of America, Endometriosis UK, Endometriosis Australia), mental health resources specialized in chronic pain, support groups, and educational platforms. You are not alone.

</details>
