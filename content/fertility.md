# Fertility

## Fertility & Endometriosis

Endometriosis is one of the leading causes of infertility, found in 25-50% of people experiencing difficulty conceiving. Understanding how it affects fertility, and the options available, is essential for informed decision-making.

## How Endometriosis Affects Fertility

### Mechanisms

- **Inflammation:** Chronic pelvic inflammation creates a hostile environment for eggs, sperm, and embryos
- **Adhesions:** Scar tissue can distort pelvic anatomy, blocking fallopian tubes or restricting ovarian function
- **Endometriomas:** Ovarian cysts ("chocolate cysts") can damage healthy ovarian tissue and reduce egg reserve
- **Hormonal disruption:** Altered hormonal signaling can affect ovulation and implantation
- **Immune dysfunction:** Changes in immune response may impair embryo implantation
- **Reduced egg quality:** Oxidative stress from Endometriosis may affect oocyte quality

Severity of visible disease does **not** always predict fertility outcomes. Many people with Endometriosis conceive **without** medical assistance.

## Fertility Preservation

### Egg Freezing (Oocyte Cryopreservation)

Egg freezing may be recommended if:
- You have endometriomas that may require surgery (which can reduce ovarian reserve)
- You're not ready for pregnancy but have declining ovarian reserve (AMH levels)
- You're planning future Endometriosis surgery
- You want to preserve options while managing your condition

**Key considerations:**
- Best outcomes when done before age 35
- Typically requires 1-2 weeks of hormone injections and monitoring
- Multiple cycles may be needed to bank enough eggs
- Discuss timing with both your Endometriosis specialist and reproductive endocrinologist

### Embryo Freezing

If you have a partner or want to use donor sperm, embryo freezing may offer higher success rates per frozen sample compared to egg freezing.

## Assisted Reproduction

### IVF (In Vitro Fertilization)

IVF is the most common assisted reproduction technology for Endometriosis-related infertility:

- **Success rates** for people with Endometriosis are generally good, though may be slightly lower than for other causes of infertility
- **Suppression protocols:** Some clinics use 2-3 months of GnRH agonist suppression before IVF to improve outcomes
- **Endometrioma management:** Surgical drainage before IVF is controversial; discuss pros and cons with your team
- **Multiple cycles** may be needed; this is normal

### IUI (Intrauterine Insemination)

- May be appropriate for mild Endometriosis with open fallopian tubes
- Often combined with ovulation-stimulating medications
- Lower success rates than IVF but less invasive and less expensive
- Typically 3-6 cycles are attempted before moving to IVF

### Surgery Before Fertility Treatment

- Excision surgery may improve natural conception rates, especially for mild-moderate Endometriosis
- Removing endometriomas requires careful technique to preserve ovarian tissue
- Discuss the balance between surgical benefits and potential ovarian damage with your surgeon
- Timing between surgery and IVF is important; most specialists recommend proceeding within 6-12 months

## Natural Conception

Many people with Endometriosis conceive naturally. Tips that may help:

- **Track ovulation:** Use ovulation predictor kits, basal body temperature, or cervical mucus monitoring
- **Optimize timing:** Intercourse in the 5 days before and day of ovulation
- **Manage inflammation:** Anti-inflammatory diet and supplements (consult your doctor)
- **Reduce stress:** Easier said than done, but chronic stress can affect fertility hormones
- **Seek early evaluation:** If under 35, seek fertility evaluation after 6 months of trying (not 12 months, given endo)

## Pregnancy with Endometriosis

### What to Expect

- Pregnancy often (but not always) temporarily improves Endometriosis symptoms due to high progesterone
- Some people experience worsening symptoms, particularly in the first trimester
- Endometriosis may increase risk of: preterm birth, placenta previa, cesarean delivery, and miscarriage
- Adenomyosis may increase risk of preterm labor and postpartum hemorrhage

### After Delivery

- Symptoms commonly return after delivery, especially once menstruation resumes
- Breastfeeding may delay symptom return due to continued hormonal suppression
- Discuss postpartum management plans with your specialist before delivery

## Finding a Fertility Specialist

Look for a **reproductive endocrinologist (RE)** who has specific experience with Endometriosis:

- [SART](https://www.sart.org/): Society for Assisted Reproductive Technology (find clinics, compare success rates)
- [ASRM](https://www.asrm.org/): American Society for Reproductive Medicine
- [Fertility Network UK](https://fertilitynetworkuk.org/): UK-based fertility support
- [Access Australia](https://www.accessaustralia.org.au/): Australian fertility support
- Ask your Endometriosis specialist for referrals to REs they trust

## Emotional Support

The intersection of Endometriosis and fertility struggles can be emotionally devastating:

- **Grief is valid:** Mourning the loss of expected fertility is a real and important process
- **Couples counseling:** Fertility challenges strain relationships; professional support helps
- [RESOLVE](https://resolve.org/): National Infertility Association (US) with support groups and resources
- [Fertility Network UK](https://fertilitynetworkuk.org/): Free support line and counseling
- Online communities like r/infertility and r/IVF provide peer support

## Key Research

- Endometriosis treatment before IVF may improve outcomes ([Cochrane Review](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/))
- Long-acting GnRH agonist before IVF increases live birth rates in moderate-severe Endometriosis
- Conservative surgery improves spontaneous pregnancy rates in early-stage Endometriosis
- AMH testing helps assess ovarian reserve and guide fertility planning
